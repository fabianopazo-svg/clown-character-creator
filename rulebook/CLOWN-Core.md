## WELCOME TO THE SHOW

Somewhere beneath the world you know, the oldest trick in show business is still running: a room full of people believing, for one bright moment, that what they're watching is real. Most of the time it isn't. Sometimes it is.

You are a Clown. Not the birthday-party kind, not the horror-movie kind — the real kind, the kind that existed for five thousand years before either of those became a cliché. A whiteface tyrant, a red-nosed fool, a sworn avenger, a healer with a stethoscope shaped like a duck. You have a life. A job. A family, maybe, who has no idea. And you have a second life, one lived in greasepaint, where the rules of the world bend around a joke told well enough.

This is a game about laughter as fuel, failure as a weapon, and the terrible, wonderful cost of being seen. You will build a party of Clowns, choose the tradition and the craft that shape your power, and go out into a city that doesn't know it's haunted by its own oldest art form.

The show must go on. It always has.

# CHAPTER 1: THE WORLD

## Two Cities, One Address

The world you grew up in is real. The bus routes, the rent, the argument you had with your sister last week — all of it is exactly as it seems. Layered on top of it, invisible to nearly everyone, is **the Backstage**: a liminal realm that mirrors the mortal world and connects it through hidden doors.

The Backstage is not a place so much as a *condition*. It exists behind every stage that was ever really used, every circus ring, every street corner where someone once did a card trick for spare change. These places are called **Thresholds**, and a Clown who knows enough of them can move through the Backstage faster than any mortal road allows. Time behaves strangely there — a scene played out in the Backstage can feel like an evening, but it never costs more than a single night back in the mortal world. The two cities stay in sync, mostly, and everyone who lives in both is grateful for that mercy.

## What a Clown Is

A Clown is not a costume. It's a covenant.

Somewhere in your life — in one enormous act of courage, or in a thousand small ones nobody but you noticed — you did something that mattered. Somebody was watching. Not a person, exactly. A **Troupe**: one of seven old traditions of the craft, each one a living current of the real history of clowning, each one capable of recognizing its own kind. They left you a sign. A scrap of red fabric. A word chalked somewhere only you would find it. Most people who receive the sign never understand it. A few feel the pull anyway, and follow it into the Backstage, where they build a second self: a **Clown persona**, invented, chosen, and entirely their own.

Putting on the paint and the clothes is called **taking the face**. While you wear it — fully or in part — you have access to your gifts. Without it, you're just a person who happens to know a secret.

## Laughter, Face, and the Deal You Never Signed

Everything a Clown can do runs on **Laughter** — not applause exactly, but the real thing: a moment where someone's guard drops and something true gets through, whether that's joy, discomfort, or grief turned inside out. You generate it by performing, for real, in front of someone. You spend it to do the impossible.

But there's a cost beneath the cost. Underneath Laughter sits **Face** — who you actually are, under the paint. Cruelty spends it. Failing alone, with no one to catch the fall and turn it into something funny, spends it too. Lose all your Face and you don't die. You get stuck. The mask stops coming off, the person under it stops answering, and what's left behind becomes an empty performance that everyone else in the Backstage learns to avoid.

## Costumes Older Than You

A Clown's look — the paint, the clothes, the specific shape of the persona — is called a **Motley**. Most Clowns build their own from nothing. Some inherit one, or take one from a rival they've beaten badly enough. And some Motleys are older than anyone currently wearing them: passed hand to hand, generation to generation, each one carrying a little of everyone who ever wore it before.

Taking on a dead Clown's Motley for good is called **Incorporation**, and it is never safe. The stronger the Motley's original owner was, the more of them is still folded into the fabric — and if you fail to hold your own Face against that weight, the original doesn't just haunt you. They *take the wheel*. Your face becomes theirs. Your memories become theirs to keep or discard. Nobody else notices the switch, because as far as the world is concerned, this was always their body. This is how some of the oldest, most powerful Clowns in the Backstage have quietly outlived every mortal lifespan they were ever born into.

## Craft Runs in Families — Carefully

Two Clowns of different Troupes can fall in love, marry, and raise a child, and if both parents keep their vow of silence, that child may one day receive the Calling on their own — carrying a natural gift for both traditions. This is called being **Tent-Born**.

The silence is not optional. Telling a child about the Backstage before their own Calling arrives poisons the gift entirely; it can never come to them after that. So Tent-Born households live with an ache most outsiders never guess at: two parents who cannot explain why they vanish some nights, hoping their child finds the truth on their own, in their own time, for their own reasons.

## The Mortal World Is Watching, Even When It Isn't

Every piece of magic a Clown performs has two faces. To the Backstage, it's real: an actual, dangerous, reality-bending act. To a mortal standing three feet away, it needs to look like something else entirely — a magic trick, a lucky pratfall, an oddly well-timed prop gag. This dual nature is called **Double Vision**: the **Turn** is what really happened, and the **Bit** is the story you sell to cover it.

Sell it well, and nobody remembers anything strange. Sell it badly, and you generate **Suspicion** — pressure that builds in a place or a scene until something with real authority comes to clean it up (see **The Ushers**, Chapter 9). Mortal fear of clowns — the whole modern phenomenon of it — isn't an old tradition at all. It's a symptom: generations of botched cover stories, half-seen and half-remembered, curdling into a folk nightmare that has nothing to do with what the craft actually is.

# CHAPTER 2: CORE MECHANICS

## The Roll

Whenever a Clown attempts something with real risk, roll a pool of ten-sided dice (d10s) equal to an **Attribute** plus a **Skill** (see Chapter 6), plus one die for each applicable **Specialty**.

**Each die showing 8, 9, or 10 is a success.** The number of successes tells you whether the action works and how well.

## The House Die

Alongside your pool, roll one **House Die** — a single d12 of a different color. It does not add to your pool. It answers a different question entirely: not *did it work*, but *how does the world take it*.

The MC sets a **House Difficulty** modifier before the roll, added to or subtracted from the House Die, based on how easy the audience in that scene is to win over — a birthday party full of children is far more forgiving than a hostile crowd, a funeral, or a skeptical critic.

- **7–12 (after modifiers) = Cheers.** The room is with you.
- **1–6 = Jeers.** The room turns cold.

## Two Ways to Push a Roll

### The Creative Gambit
Before rolling, describe an unusually bold or clever approach to the action — more than just "I try harder." If the table agrees it genuinely earns it, add **+2 dice** to your pool.

The cost: if the result is any kind of Failure, you lose **1 additional Face**, on top of anything else. This is how a low-ranked Clown can, in a single dramatic moment, out-perform a legend — but it isn't free, and it isn't meant to be used on every roll.

### The Encore
After seeing your results, spend **1 Laughter per die** to reroll up to 3 dice from your pool that didn't score a success. New results replace the old ones on those dice only. The House Die is never rerolled this way.

The Creative Gambit changes your odds before you know anything; the Encore lets you react to a result you've already seen, at a fixed and certain cost.

## Turn and Bit

Any supernatural Gift used in front of mortals needs both halves of **Double Vision**:

- **The Turn** is what actually happened — the real mechanical effect, resolved with your dice pool as normal.
- **The Bit** is the story you tell to cover it — the magic trick, the pratfall, the "lucky" prop gag that explains what a mortal bystander just witnessed.

Selling the Bit well (a Cheers result) keeps things clean. Selling it badly (Jeers, especially Corpsing) raises Suspicion. In the Backstage itself, with no mortals present, there's no Bit to sell — Turns simply happen as they are.
## The Result Matrix

Failure is never neutral in this game. It's either the best moment of the scene or the most dangerous one, and the dice — not the player — decide which. Cross your pool result against your House Die result:

| | **Cheers** | **Jeers** |
|---|---|---|
| **Success** (1+ successes) | Clean success. Gain **1 Laughter**; a Critical Success (3+ successes) also grants the party **Momentum**. | It works, but the MC gains **1 Suspicion** — a witness, a cost, an uncomfortable complication. |
| **Failure** (0 successes) | **The Flop.** It didn't work — but it was glorious. Narrate the disaster; if the table genuinely laughs, gain **1–2 Laughter** anyway. | **Corpsing.** It didn't work and it landed badly. The MC gains **2 Suspicion**. The only fully bad result on the table. |

## The Resources

### Laughter (0–10) — your fuel
Every active Gift costs Laughter to use. You earn it by making someone laugh for real, by landing a good Flop, and by performing for an audience (see Chapter 4). It does not refill on its own — a Clown who never performs for anyone runs dry.

### Face (1–10) — your soul
Face erodes when you use your Gifts cruelly or selfishly, and when you fail badly with no one there to catch it. It can be restored through genuine acts of generosity, resolved emotional arcs, and — for one specific Path — real care given to someone in need. **At 0 Face, a character doesn't die. They get stuck in the mask permanently** — the only true "death" condition in this game.

### Renown (1–10) — your legend
Renown is both your character level and a measure of how deeply your name is carved into the Backstage's memory. It never gets spent; it only rises, through deeds that the Backstage genuinely comes to know and repeat as legend. Renown determines your rank (see Chapter 4) and, crucially, how much of a target you've become: a Clown with high Renown who falls leaves behind a far more valuable, and far more dangerous, Motley for someone else to claim.

### Suspicion — the MC's pressure gauge
A shared, table-visible counter the MC gains from Jeers and Corpsing, and spends to activate boss abilities, introduce complications, or summon the Ushers.

### Momentum — the party's shared spark
Gained from Critical Successes with Cheers, and from certain Gifts (especially the Host's). Spent collectively — to boost another player's roll, to trigger a joint Turn between two or more Clowns, or to power a coordinated finishing move against a tough enemy.

## Calibration Benchmarks

A few fixed numbers that keep every table running the same game.

**Damage.** A Turn that connects deals harm equal to its successes, minus the defender's soak if they chose to Take the Hit. This reduces the appropriate track — Health, Laughter, or Face — by that many boxes (minimum 0). Group or area Gifts that don't specify an amount deal a flat 1–2 boxes per target regardless of successes, unless the Gift says otherwise.

**Health.** A character's Health track has a number of boxes equal to **3 + Toughness**. At half or fewer boxes remaining, act at −1 die on physical rolls; on the last box, −2 die. Emptying it drops a character out of the fight, with no lasting cost.

**Extended rolls.** When a task takes real in-fiction time, successes accumulate roll by roll toward a target the MC sets before the first roll:

| Task scale | Successes needed |
|---|---|
| Quick (a few minutes) | 5 |
| Standard (an hour or an evening) | 8 |
| Difficult (days) | 12 |
| Major (restoring a Motley, cracking deep Backstage secrets) | 18–20 |

A Flop on an extended roll never erases progress — it complicates the path forward instead.

**Opposed rolls.** Both sides roll their pool; compare successes. Higher total wins; ties favor whoever is defending or resisting.

**Building an NPC on the fly.** If a threat isn't in the Bestiary, give it a dice pool of roughly **its Renown-equivalent + 2**, and use the Quick Scaling table (Chapter 10) for Health, Laughter, Face, and known Gifts.

**Suspicion and Momentum, over time.** Suspicion doesn't vanish on its own — it persists for the location or storyline it's tied to until the party addresses it or the Ushers do. Left completely unaddressed, it drops by 1 at the start of each new Scene, so a single bad Bit doesn't haunt a campaign forever, but a pattern of them will. Momentum caps at **5** for the party and doesn't decay within an Act, but resets to 0 at the start of a new one.

**Incorporation and Possession.** When a Clown attempts to permanently claim a dead Clown's Motley (Incorporation), or when a Shackled Heir's Debt is collected, resolve it as an opposed roll: the claimant rolls a dice pool equal to their **Face rating** (their Face score itself, treated as a number of d10s — not Attribute + Skill), while the Motley's original owner rolls a pool equal to their **Renown rating** at the time of death (or their current Renown, for a living Patron). Compare successes as a normal opposed roll (8-9-10 on each die). If the claimant scores equal or more successes, the Incorporation succeeds cleanly and they keep themselves. If the original owner scores more, **Possession** occurs: the spirit takes the wheel (see Chapter 1). This roll cannot be aided by Specialties, the Creative Gambit, or the Encore — it is a contest between the two Face/Renown ratings alone, nothing else.

# CHAPTER 3: STRUCTURE OF PLAY



A session of this game is called a **Show**, broken into **Acts**, made of **Scenes**, moved forward turn by turn through something called **the Spotlight**. Between sessions, the game rests in **Intermission**.

## The Spotlight (there is no fixed turn order)

There is no numeric initiative. Instead, **the Spotlight** moves narratively: whoever has the natural reaction to what just happened, acts next.

When a player **fails a roll with Jeers** (a Jeer-Failure, i.e. Corpsing), the Spotlight passes to the MC, who may spend Suspicion to let an enemy act. In other words: **the players' bad rolls are what activate their enemies.** The MC can also spend Suspicion at any time to seize the Spotlight outright — an interruption, a collapse, a sudden reversal.

While you hold the Spotlight, you get **one Main Action** (a Turn, an attack, a significant move, a complex skill use) and as many reasonable **Minor Actions** as fit the moment (a line, a gesture, maintaining a Bit, switching a Stance).

## Scene, Act, Show, Intermission

- **A Scene** is a unit of focused action — a place, a conflict, a goal. It starts when attention locks onto something and ends when that something resolves or the story cuts away. Most "once per scene" effects reset here.
- **An Act** is a block of several scenes forming its own arc within the Show. Some effects reset "at the end of the Act."
- **A Show** is a full session. Effects that trigger "once per Show" (several Path and Troupe capstones) refer to this. At the start of a Show, Hosts and Illusionists prepare their **Repertoire** (Chapter 7).
- **Intermission** is everything that happens between sessions or major Acts: Face recovers slowly here, Renown gains are formalized, Motleys are maintained, Debts are paid down, Troupe politics move forward. Intermission never plays out live within a Scene.

# CHAPTER 4: THE FOUR MODES OF PLAY

## Combat

Combat mode begins whenever there's active opposition and the order of actions genuinely matters.

- **The Spotlight** governs turn order (see Chapter 3).
- **Resolving an attack:** the attacker rolls their pool plus the House Die. The defender either **Dodges** (an opposed Grace + Acrobatics roll — success negates the hit) or **Takes the Hit** (subtracting their **Toughness** skill as flat soak from the damage).
- **Three separate defeat tracks** — and it matters which one you empty:
  - **Physical Health** — knocked out of the fight bodily, no lasting cost, recovers with time.
  - **Laughter** — drained dry, a tactical knockout that locks out Gifts until recharged, often within the same session.
  - **Face** — an existential break. **Only emptying an opponent's Face opens the door to Seizing their Motley.**
- **Open Combat** happens in the Backstage, with no mortals present — no Bit required, Turns resolve directly, and fights tend to be faster and more lethal.
- **Covert Combat** happens in front of mortals — every Turn needs a Bit, and Suspicion can spike fast if a cover story lands badly mid-fight.
- **Bosses** get multiple activations per round (the MC spends Suspicion for each), distinct phases, and their own Laughter/Face/Renown tracks exactly like player characters. A party overcomes them through coordination — shared Momentum fuels the finishing blow.
- **Summons:** several Gifts (chiefly the Illusionist's) can call up temporary constructs — balloon animals, hat-pulled doves, shadow puppets — each with simple stats and its own built-in Bit.

## Exploration

Moving through the world, searching, solving obstacles, traveling.

- **A single roll** handles most one-shot actions (forcing something open, noticing a detail, recalling lore).
- **An extended roll** covers longer tasks — investigating, restoring a Motley — where several rolls accumulate successes toward a target the MC sets in advance. A Flop here doesn't erase progress; it complicates it.
- The **House Die** in exploration represents the world and luck at large rather than a literal audience — Jeers means the world turns against you, Cheers means something breaks your way.
- **Navigating the Backstage** (see below) is this game's signature form of exploration.

## Mortal-World Interaction

Social and everyday scenes among people who have no idea the Backstage exists.

- **Double Vision governs everything here.** Any Gift used needs its Bit. Without taking the face, a Clown has only their base Attributes and Skills — no Gifts at all.
- **Performing for an audience** is the primary way to refill Laughter, and the mortal world is where the audiences are. A Clown who hides from the world runs out of fuel.
- **Suspicion is the danger here** — it builds from botched Bits, and high enough Suspicion draws the Ushers (Chapter 9) or feeds the mortal world's fear of clowns.
- **Ordinary social interaction** — persuasion, deception — resolves with standard opposed rolls; Double Vision only enters the picture when a Gift is actually involved.
- The **House Difficulty** of a mortal audience sets the tone of the scene: a child is easy, a hostile official is not.

## In the Backstage

The hidden, navigable plane where a Clown's magic is visible and literal, invisible to mortals.

- **Access is through Thresholds** — places where a real performance once happened. The more Thresholds a Clown knows, the faster they move beneath the mortal world.
- **No Double Vision is needed** here — there are no mortals to fool, so combat is Open Combat, cleaner and deadlier.
- **A hard rule of time:** a full scene spent in the Backstage never costs more than a single night back in the mortal world, no matter how much subjective time passes inside it.
- **Navigation** requires no roll unless a Threshold is unstable, watched, or a shortcut is being sought (Knowledge + Trade Lore).
- **Who lives here:** Ring Legends (Renown 9–10 Clowns, some of them centuries-old chains of Incorporation), the Ushers, and the lingering spirits of unclaimed Motleys. This is the natural territory of boss encounters.

# CHAPTER 5: ATTRIBUTES, SKILLS, AND PERSONAL DETAIL

## The Six Attributes (1–5)

| Attribute | Governs |
|---|---|
| **Strength** | Raw physical power, weighty pratfalls, how hard a hit lands |
| **Grace** | Agility, physical timing, juggling, controlled falls, dodging |
| **Charisma** | Magnetism, the pull that makes people want to watch you |
| **Poise** | Composure under pressure, holding the Face together |
| **Wit** | Mental speed, comic timing, reading a room fast, quick deception |
| **Knowledge** | Learning, memory, long-term planning |

## The Nine Skills (0–5) and Performance

| Skill | Typical Attribute | Covers |
|---|---|---|
| Brawl | Strength | Hand-to-hand combat, a pratfall with real weight |
| Acrobatics | Grace | Juggling, controlled falls, dodging |
| Toughness | — (not rolled for soak) | How much punishment the body absorbs; used as flat soak in combat |
| Persuasion | Charisma | Convincing, charming, selling an idea |
| Subterfuge | Wit | Deceiving, stealing, misdirection |
| Perception | Wit or Knowledge | Mundane awareness: details, body language, ordinary lies |
| Awareness | Wit or Poise | The craft's sixth sense: sensing rising Suspicion, a failing Bit, a nearby Threshold or power |
| Trade Lore | Knowledge | Knowledge of the hidden world: Troupes, Ring Legends, the Backstage's own history |
| Scholarship | Knowledge | General knowledge, planning, book learning |

Every Clown has **Performance**, and it lives apart from the rest: a minimum of 1 dot (nobody reaches the Backstage without *some* stagecraft), bought like any other skill up to 3 dots at creation, and — uniquely — it also rises automatically with Renown rank, regardless of points spent:

| Rank | Bonus to Performance |
|---|---|
| First of May (Renown 1–2) | +0 |
| Tent Veteran (Renown 3–4) | +1 |
| Ring Name (Renown 5–6) | +2 |
| Backstage Legend (Renown 7–8) | +3 |
| Ring Legend (Renown 9–10) | +4 |

A legendary Clown is, almost by definition, a master of the stage — fame sharpens the craft on its own. Performance pairs with whichever Attribute fits the moment: Charisma for pure charm, Wit for a con, Knowledge for an elaborate setup.

## Specialties

Each character begins with **2–4 Specialties**: short, freely written tags describing something concrete about their human life or background that none of the nine skills quite covers — *"Pediatric ICU nurse," "Survived a hospital fire," "Grew up in a real circus."* Each grants **+1 die** when it genuinely applies (an exceptionally deep Specialty can count as two, granting +2, but counts double against your limit).

## Personality Traits

Every character takes **2–5 Personality Traits**, drawn from the list below or negotiated with the MC, each with a small but real mechanical hook.

- **Messy / Hyperactive** — takes the face in one minute instead of five, but must roll Perception to remember every prop; on a failure, the look comes out incomplete.
- **Perfectionist** — never comes out incomplete by accident, but can never rush getting into character.
- **Melancholic** — +1 die generating Laughter from genuine sadness or vulnerability; −1 die with pure slapstick nonsense.
- **Loudmouth** — +1 die to Subterfuge when bluffing on the spot; higher risk of Corpsing if it fails.
- **Private** — generates less Suspicion by default; generates Laughter more slowly.
- **Control Freak** — +1 die on Gifts that impose order or structure; a Flop in a chaotic situation costs an extra point of Face.
- **Generous** — recovers Face faster from genuine generosity resolved in play; loses it faster when a Gift is used selfishly.
- **Reckless Bravery** — one Creative Gambit per session doesn't need table approval; its Face cost on failure doubles.

## Age Bands

| Band | Age range | Bonus | Penalty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Young Adult** | 18–38 | +1 Grace | −1 Poise |
| **Adult** | 39–59 | +1 Poise | −1 Strength |
| **Elder** | 60+ | +1 Knowledge | −1 Grace |

Physical vitality peaks young and fades; composure and accumulated knowledge climb with age. No band is objectively better — each trades something real for something real.

## Tent-Born

A character raised by two Clown parents of different Troupes chooses **one parent's Troupe as their own** — full membership, full passives, no different from any other Clown of that Troupe. From the *other* parent, they inherit a single **Heritage Trait**: a small, always-on nudge, not membership. A Tent-Born character never gets two full sets of Troupe passives — just their one Troupe, plus this one flourish from the household they also grew up in.

| Secondary Troupe | Heritage Trait |
|---|---|
| The Whiteface | *A Natural Air of Command* — +1 die when giving someone a direction and expecting it followed |
| The Auguste | *Grew Up Falling Down* — +1 die to Acrobatics for pratfalls and controlled falls |
| The School of Failure | *Raised on Flops* — once per session, downgrade a Corpsing result to a Flop |
| The Laughter Doctors | *A Gentle Hand* — +1 die when comforting or calming someone in real distress |
| The Rebel Clown Army | *Knows Power When It Sees It* — always know who in a scene actually holds institutional authority |
| The Tramp | *Traveled Light* — one additional Specialty at creation, reflecting a hardscrabble upbringing |
| The New Circus | *Stage-Trained Young* — +1 die to Performance in any prepared or staged venue |


## Money — the Purse

A Clown still has to live. Track wealth with a single number, your **Purse**, rated in **Coin**. It isn't a detailed budget — the MC only asks for Coin when it actually matters to the story (a bribe, a rare prop, a month's rent falling due at a bad time), never as a running tally of groceries.

**Starting Purse** is set by your age band, reflecting how much of a mortal foothold you've had time to build:

| Age Band | Starting Purse |
|---|---|
| Young Adult | 20 Coin |
| Adult | 35 Coin |
| Elder | 50 Coin |

## Gear of the Trade

Every item has a **Type** (how it works), a **Rarity** (how hard it is to find or afford), a **Size** (how easy it is to carry or conceal), and a **Cost** in Coin.

**Types:** **Passive** (an always-on bonus while worn or carried), **Active** (requires a roll, but isn't consumed), **Consumable** (used up, sometimes rechargeable in the Backstage).
**Rarities:** **Common** (any hardware store or costume shop), **Uncommon** (a specialty supplier, or a favor), **Rare** (tied to a Troupe's own network, or genuinely hard to find), **Legendary** (not for sale at any price — found, earned, or inherited).
**Sizes:** **Tiny** (a pocket), **Small** (a bag), **Bulky** (needs both hands, hard to hide), **Large** (needs a vehicle or a stage to bring along).

### Troupe Gear

Rarer props tied to a specific Troupe's own networks — easiest to find or be gifted through that Troupe, though nothing stops another Clown from acquiring one if the story allows it.

# CHAPTER 6: BUILDING A CLOWN

## Creation, Step by Step

1. **Human concept.** Who is the person underneath — job, relationships, a recognizable mortal life.
2. **The Calling.** A single defining act, or a lifetime of small ones? Which Troupe answered (or, if Tent-Born, which Troupe do you claim as your own)?
3. **Age Band.** Choose Young Adult, Adult, or Elder, and apply the bonus/penalty.
4. **Attributes.** Distribute 10–12 dots across the six Attributes (max 4 dots at creation), then apply your age band's modifier.
5. **Skills, Performance, and Specialties.** Spend 14 points across the nine Skills (1 point per dot) and Performance (2 points per dot above the free minimum of 1). Then choose 2–4 Specialties.
6. **Troupe and Path.** Choose your Troupe (your Founding passive activates immediately) and your starting Path, including a Subtype and your first Grade I Gifts (see Chapter 7 for the acquisition rule). Starting within one of your Troupe's Affinity Paths keeps you eligible for the **True to the Craft** bonus (Chapter 7) — you can always branch out later, but that bonus ends the moment you do.
7. **The Clown.** Design the persona itself — ring name, look, makeup, costume, and your relationship to taking the face.
8. **Starting resources.** Laughter = Poise + Charisma. Face = 7 (default). Renown = 1. Any Path-specific resource starts empty or at its listed default.
9. **Backstory.** Answer three questions: *Who left your first cryptic sign, and do you still have it? Who are you keeping the truth from? What Motley besides your own have you ever worn, and what did it cost?*

## Learning Gifts

At **Renown 1**, choose **3 Gifts** from your Path's Grade I pool. At each Renown point from **2 through 9**, choose **1 additional Gift** from any unlocked Grade whose depth prerequisite you meet (see Chapter 7). At **Renown 10**, choose **1 of 2 capstone Gifts** from Grade V. This totals 11 regular Gifts plus one capstone.

## Worked Example: Joaquin Reyes

**Step 1.** Joaquin Reyes, 45, a pediatric nurse — quiet, watchful, the kind of person who remembers every patient's name. Nobody at work suspects he's had a red nose in his locker for years.

**Step 3.** **Adult** (39–59): +1 Poise, −1 Strength.

**Step 4.** 12 dots at creation, age modifier applied after:

|---|---|---|---|
| Poise | 3 | +1 | **4** |
| Knowledge | 3 | — | **3** |
| Charisma | 2 | — | **2** |
| Wit | 1 | — | **1** |
| Strength | 2 | −1 | **1** |

**Step 5.** Performance bought to 2 dots (2 points). Remaining 12 points: Perception 3, Persuasion 2, Awareness 2, Scholarship 2, Toughness 1, Brawl 1, Trade Lore 1. Specialties: *"Pediatric ICU nurse," "Survived a hospital fire," "Speaks gently to the frightened."*

**Step 6.** Troupe: **The Laughter Doctors** (Founding passive active: **Clinical Eye** — you sense, without a roll, when someone nearby is close to losing their Face entirely). # CHAPTER 7: THE SEVEN PATHS

A **Path** is your character's class — how your power actually works. Any Troupe can walk any Path, though each Troupe has **Affinities**: 2–3 Paths, reflecting which crafts historically ran in that tradition, that reward staying within them (see True to the Craft, below).

**The depth prerequisite:** to learn a Gift of Grade N in a Path, you must already know at least (N–1) Gifts of that same Path. This stops anyone from leaping straight to a Path's high-end power without walking the road first.

**True to the Craft — the affinity bonus, quantified.** You may learn Gifts from any Path in the game, following only the depth prerequisite above — there's no restriction and no extra cost to stepping outside your Troupe's tradition. But as long as **every single Gift you currently know** belongs to one of your Troupe's Affinity Paths, you carry **True to the Craft**: **+1 die** on every roll that activates one of those Gifts, and their listed Laughter/Debt/Comfort/Cards/Conviction cost is reduced by **1** (minimum 1). The instant you learn your first Gift from a Path outside your Troupe's Affinities, True to the Craft is lost — permanently, since a Clown never unlearns a Gift once taken. Breadth is always available to anyone; staying true to your tradition is what's rewarded.

**Default cost rule.** Unless a Gift says otherwise, its listed cost is paid **before** the roll, from the resource named in its Path's economy (Laughter for most Paths):

- **Debt** or Laughter for the Shackled.
- **Comfort** for the Physician.
- **Cards** for the Pickpocket.
- **Conviction** for the Sworn.

A Gift marked **Passive** costs nothing and is simply always active while its conditions are met.

## THE ILLUSIONIST

The scholar of the craft — magic tricks treated like grimoires. Primary Attributes: **Wit** and **Knowledge**.

**Schools of Illusion, not fixed spells.** Instead of a spell list, the Illusionist learns broad **Schools**, each with a **Domain** (what it can do) and a **Limit** (what it explicitly cannot) — the Limit is what makes each School distinct and forces real creativity within a clear boundary. Casting from a School costs Laughter as listed by its Grade (below); **combining two Schools** in one act costs the sum of both plus **+1 Laughter**, and raises the difficulty by treating the roll as one Grade harder to sell. Advancing a Grade can also be spent on **Mastery** — permanently loosening one known School's Limit — instead of learning a new School.

**Subtypes:** **The Sleight-of-Hand Artist** (close-up precision), **The Escape Artist** (mobility and evasion), **The Conjurer** (summoning).

## THE SHACKLED

Power that isn't yours — borrowed from something larger in the Backstage, with a debt always attached. The mechanical opposite of the Illusionist: fixed, rigid, potent effects instead of free creativity. Primary Attributes: **Poise**, plus Charisma or Wit depending on the Patron.

**Debt, not a spending pool.** Every Prerogative below lists two costs: a Laughter cost (clean, no consequence) and a Debt cost (fiat — marked on your 0–10 Debt track, always payable even at 0 Laughter). Debt doesn't hurt until it crosses thresholds — then **the Patron collects**.

## THE PHYSICIAN

Laughter channeled as literal care. The only Path that repairs another character's Face. Primary Attributes: **Poise** and **Knowledge**.

**Comfort, generated backwards.** Every other Path recharges by performing for a crowd. ## THE STRONGMAN

The game's simplest, most direct Path — deliberately so, and the best entry point for a new player. No spending pool to track beyond Laughter: just a chosen **Stance** and rising **Grit**. Primary Attributes: **Strength** and **Toughness**.

**Stance (free, chosen, no cost, switch as a minor action):** **Attack Stance** (+2 dice to hit, −2 dice to Dodge), **Guard Stance** (redirect a hit meant for an adjacent ally, +2 to soak, −2 dice to hit), **Grit Stance** (halve all incoming damage after soak, but only a minor action is available this turn — no Main Action).

Several Gifts below amplify this further. The Strongman about to fall is the most dangerous person at the table.

**Subtypes:** **The Powerhouse** (offense, brute force), **The Wall** (protection), **The Rubber Clown** (resilience and counterattack).

## THE SWORN

The one who made a vow, and draws real power from keeping it. Primary Attributes: **Charisma** and **Poise**.

**The Oath, declared and specific.** Not "be good" — a real, playable vow declared at creation, defining your Subtype, what generates Conviction, and what counts as betrayal.

**Conviction, earned by acting in character — literally.** The only resource in the game generated purely by roleplay, not rolls or a crowd. Guideline for the table: award **1 Conviction** for a small, genuine act in service of the Oath within a scene; **2–3 Conviction** for a costly or campaign-defining act of devotion to it. Conviction is spent on the Gifts below.

**Auras.** While conscious and in character, a Sworn projects a passive zone of benefit for nearby allies, shaped by their Oath (see Subtypes). It costs nothing to maintain; spending Conviction on **Aura Ablaze** or **Living Banner** intensifies or extends it.

**Oathbreaking.** Acting against your own vow drops all Conviction to zero, disables the Aura, and locks out Conviction-fueled Gifts until an act of **atonement** — negotiated with the table — restores the Oath, or a new Oath is sworn.

**Subtypes (Oaths):**

# CHAPTER 8: THE SEVEN TROUPES

A **Troupe** is your character's tradition — the living current of clowning history that answered your Calling. Each Troupe grants four passive traits that unlock as your Renown rises: **Founding** (Renown 1), **Affinity** (Renown 3, amplified if you walk one of the Troupe's Affinity Paths), **Recognition** (Renown 6), and **Legend** (Renown 9). Unless stated otherwise, a passive costs no Laughter and needs no roll — it simply applies when its trigger occurs.

---

## THE WHITEFACE

**Values:** discipline, earned craft, formal authority, the laughter that costs something to win. **Look:** full white face and neck, red-tipped ears, formal and immaculate dress — nothing out of place by accident. **Affinities:** The Sworn, The Host, The Illusionist.

The oldest and most structured tradition, tracing back to the ringmasters of the classic circus. They believe a laugh that costs nothing is barely worth having, and they run their own ranks like a proper institution — mentors take apprentices, promotion is a real ceremony. At their best, they lead without crushing, drawing out the best in an ensemble through real discipline. At their worst, "tradition" becomes an excuse for cruelty toward those they consider beneath them — a direct echo of real circus history's ugliest double acts.

**Founding — Ring Authority.** Once per scene, +2 dice to a Persuasion or Intimidation roll against a mortal or Clown of lower Renown than you; if the target is a mortal of low or middling social status, the roll succeeds automatically instead of requiring a check.

**Affinity — Director's Eye.** Sense, at a glance and without a roll, when someone nearby is "performing" (lying, faking a role, covering a botched Bit) — this only tells you *that* they're performing, not what's true underneath. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, once per scene you may also lower House Difficulty by 1 whenever you're the one directing the moment.

**Recognition — Ring Reputation.** Other Clowns with Trade Lore 2+ recognize your name and rank on sight, no roll needed.

**Legend.** Once per session, **The Master's Word**: issue a single order that no mortal present can refuse, and that a Clown of lower Renown than you can only refuse by spending 1 Face. Permanently, allies within your Aura-equivalent presence (adjacent, or in the same small space) gain +2 dice to resist fear and manipulation.

**Relationships:** Locked in an old, unacknowledged mutual dependency with the Augustes. Publicly dismissive of the School of Failure, privately fascinated. Sees the New Circus as the force stripping the craft of its old prestige.

---

## THE AUGUSTES

**Values:** genuine spontaneity, failure as the whole point, closeness to ordinary people over prestige. **Look:** flesh-toned base makeup, exaggerated red-and-black features, a red ball nose, deliberately mismatched clothing. **Affinities:** The Strongman, The Pickpocket, The Host.

The most numerous Troupe by far — the most recognizable clown archetype in the mortal world. Warm, impulsive, generous almost to a fault, they carry old resentment toward the Whiteface (historically "the one who takes the pie," never the one who calls the shot) — and, secretly, many still crave that approval.

**Founding.** Two traits: **Beloved of the Crowd** (mortals of low or middling status warm to you unusually fast, no roll needed) and **Off-Script** (+2 dice resisting any Gift that would impose structure or control on you).

**Affinity — Accumulated Chaos.** Each genuine rule you break in a scene — social, physical, someone else's plan — grants a **stacking +1 die**, up to a maximum of **+3**, to your next roll; this resets at the end of the Act. If the chaos genuinely hurts someone real (not just embarrasses or inconveniences them), you lose Face at double the normal rate for that action. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, each stack also converts directly into 1 point of that Path's own resource (a Card, a point of Debt paid down, a level of Grit, etc. — MC's call for the specific Path) in addition to the die bonus.

**Recognition — Hallway Legend.** Untraceable, unmistakable, word-of-mouth fame anywhere you go — no formal reputation, but someone always seems to have heard of you.

**Legend.** Once per session, turn an ally's Corpsing into a Flop instead. Permanently, your presence lowers House Difficulty by 1 in festive or chaotic scenes for the whole party.

**Relationships:** Dependency and resentment with the Whiteface. Mutual awe with the School of Failure. The most painful tension is with the New Circus, which has steadily shrunk the clown's role in its own spotlight.

---

## THE SCHOOL OF FAILURE

**Values:** radical honesty earned through public failure, distrust of cheap laughs, near-monastic devotion to teaching. **Look:** deliberately unglamorous rehearsal clothes; the only shared marker is a small red nose worn with something close to reverence. **Affinities:** The Shackled, The Illusionist, The Pickpocket.

They believe an audience never laughs at the joke — they laugh at the fool who had a terrible idea and failed to pull it off, undefended, in front of everyone. Their highest honor isn't "Ring Legend" — internally, it's *"Monsieur/Madame Flop."* The same pedagogy that teaches real shame-release can, without warning, curdle into real cruelty dressed up as rigor — and the Troupe knows it, and hasn't agreed where the line sits.

**Founding.** Two traits: **Failure Pays More** (a sold Flop grants you 1 extra Laughter beyond the normal 1–2) and **Smell the Real Fear** (sense masked fear or shame in someone nearby, no roll needed).

**Affinity — Calculated Disaster.** After failing a roll (0 successes) where the House Die lands in Jeers territory — which would normally be a Corpsing — spend **1 Laughter** to treat that House Die result as Cheers instead, turning it into a Flop. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, you may do this once per scene for free before needing to spend Laughter.

**Recognition.** Two traits: **Master's Reputation** (recognized instantly within the Troupe's own circles) and **School Complicity** (when another School of Failure member present sells a Flop, you both gain Failure Pays More's bonus Laughter).

**Legend — Monsieur/Madame Flop.** Once per session, when you suffer a genuine, high-stakes Flop, declare that the disaster achieved your goal anyway, through no explicable means — the table agrees on the exact shape of the outcome.

**Relationships:** The truest kinship in the Backstage is with the Laughter Doctors — both believe in shared vulnerability, disagreeing only on method. Sees the New Circus's polish as a kind of betrayal of the craft's soul.

---

## THE LAUGHTER DOCTORS

**Values:** presence over cleverness, service to the vulnerable, showing up for people no one else will sit with. **Look:** modified medical whites — a lab coat with colorful patches, a doctor's bag, a quiet clown flourish. **Affinities:** The Physician, The Host, The Sworn.

Born not from the circus but from applied compassion — the practice of entering rooms where suffering concentrates and changing someone's relationship to their own pain. The most formally organized Troupe in the Backstage. Compassion fatigue is real and constant among their veterans; some worry their care has become a shield against grief they've never processed.

**Founding — Clinical Eye.** Sense, without a roll, when a nearby Face is within 2 points of hitting 0.

**Affinity — Healing Hands.** Once per scene, restore **1 Face** to a nearby ally at no Laughter or Comfort cost. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, this becomes usable twice per scene, and restores 1 Face even when used in a scene defined by real suffering (a hospital, a disaster site) without counting against your once-per-scene limit there.

**Recognition — A Coat You Trust.** Real access to spaces of suffering (hospitals, hospices, disaster zones) that would otherwise be closed to you, and even hostile Clowns hesitate to attack you mid-treatment.

**Legend.** Once per session, **No One Walks Alone**: suspend an ally about to hit 0 Face instead of losing them (as the Physician's Sit at the Threshold, usable even if you aren't a Physician). Permanently, enemies whose power feeds on fear or dread have that power reduced by 2 dice while you're present.

**Relationships:** Deep kinship with the School of Failure. Same compassionate root as the Rebel Clown Army, opposite methods. The Whiteface resents how much cultural recognition they've earned.

---

## THE REBEL CLOWN ARMY

**Values:** absurdity as resistance, refusing hierarchy, disarming power with ridicule instead of violence. **Look:** military surplus mixed with bright clown flourishes — a deliberately jarring combination; masks or face paint doubling as both performance and anonymity. **Affinities:** The Sworn, The Pickpocket, The Host.

Cell-based, leaderless by design, and always burning out or being infiltrated — the paranoia isn't madness, it's realism. They believe unjust power doesn't know how to laugh, and that's its weak point.

**Founding — The Clowns Are Organizing.** The first time each scene an authority figure (a cop, a boss, any institutionally powerful NPC) attempts to intimidate or command you, their attempt automatically fails, no roll needed. **The non-violent limit:** if you genuinely hurt an unarmed, defenseless target this scene, this passive stops working for the rest of it.

**Affinity — Against the Giants.** +2 dice against any foe of higher Renown or higher institutional status than you. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, this bonus also converts into 1 point of that Path's own resource the first time you use it in a scene.

**Recognition — The Flock Network.** Access to a clandestine network of safe houses and contacts almost anywhere. **The cost:** the MC gains a standing narrative thread representing the real risk of infiltration — expect it to surface at inconvenient moments.

**Legend.** Once per session, **Contagious Absurdity**: turn a crowd or an enemy force present into unwitting, briefly uncontrollable participants in your act — for a scene, their commanders' orders simply don't land.

**Relationships:** Direct opposition to the Whiteface. Natural sympathy with the Tramp. The Ushers are their built-in institutional enemy.

---

## THE TRAMP

**Values:** quiet endurance, dignity without spectacle, gallows humor, deep distrust of belonging to any institution — including this one. **Look:** deliberately ragged — an oversized worn coat, broken shoes, minimal makeup, often just a suggested sad mouth. **Affinities:** The Shackled, The Pickpocket, The Strongman.

Less a Troupe than a shared condition — no hierarchy, no ceremonies, no headquarters. They carry a real, uncomfortable double origin: born partly from an act of appropriation, later reclaimed as a symbol of dignity in hardship, and the Troupe has never resolved what to do with that wound.

**Founding — No One Looks Twice.** You generate half as much Suspicion as normal from a botched Bit (round down), and mortals struggle to describe or recall you accurately afterward.

**Affinity — Survive Anything.** Once per scene, ignore the first grave consequence that would drop you (a killing blow, a Face-breaking effect) — you're still standing, at 1 box/point. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, you may use this a second time in the same scene by paying 1 Face.

**Recognition — The Road's Codes.** Hidden signs, safehouses, and Backstage shortcuts other Troupes don't know exist, granting a +2 die bonus to Trade Lore or Awareness rolls made to navigate or hide in the margins of any city.

**Legend.** Once per session, **The Dignity That Won't Be Taken**: flatly and automatically negate an attempt to Seize your Motley or break your Face, no roll needed. Permanently, your Face costs enemies **+1 additional success** to reduce through any Gift or attack.

**Relationships:** Natural allies with the Rebel Clown Army. A painful mirror image with the New Circus — the two extremes of visibility and prestige.

---

## THE NEW CIRCUS

**Values:** artistic ambition, spectacle, elevating the craft past its old low-brow reputation. **Look:** high-production designer costuming, closer to an acrobat or dancer's aesthetic than a traditional clown's. **Affinities:** The Illusionist, The Host, The Strongman.

Born from the real, ironic history of the modern circus: a movement that grew out of clowning and then steadily reduced the clown's role in its own spectacles. Many of its most talented members are, technically, more acrobat than clown — and know it. The Troupe lives with a quiet dread that the soul was traded for the shine, and the bill hasn't come due yet.

**Founding — High Production.** Lower House Difficulty by 1 in any prepared or staged venue; a well-produced Bit needs no extra roll to sell if it was already prepared in advance (Repertoire or equivalent).

**Affinity — The Big Number.** Your area- or group-affecting Gifts reach 50% more targets (round up) or deal +2 dice worth of effect, whichever the Gift's design allows. If you walk one of this Troupe's Affinity Paths, add a further +1 die to that effect.

**Recognition — Marquee Name.** Real mortal-world fame, resources, and connections available as a story resource. **The cost:** you're also unusually easy for anyone — friendly or hostile — to find.

**Legend.** Once per session, **The Show of the Century**: turn a large space into your stage for a scene, affecting every mortal present at a scale no other Troupe can match (the MC and you agree on the exact shape of the effect, area, and duration).

**Relationships:** The most painful tension is with the Augustes, whom their success displaced. A mirror image with the Tramp. The School of Failure considers a flawless New Circus number a dead one.

# CHAPTER 9: THE USHERS

## The Backstage's Own Management

The Ushers are not a Troupe, and no character plays one. They are the Backstage's maintenance force — order given shape, sent to keep the Show running: erasing what leaks into the mortal world, and quietly "reassigning" any Clown who steps too far out of the script. No one built them on purpose. They emerged the moment the Backstage itself became complex enough to fear for its own survival. They are, quite literally, its antibodies.

They are not cruel. They don't enjoy any of it. They are administrative — polite, efficient, graceless, and absolutely inflexible. That impersonal, undramatic evil is what makes them unlike any other threat in this game.

## Appearance

Every Usher wears a variation of the classic theater usher's uniform: dark waistcoat, gold buttons, white shirt, bow tie, a small flashlight always at hand. Nothing flashy, nothing clownish — the aesthetic opposite of the craft they administer. Their faces are strangely forgettable, resistant to memory by design; veteran Clowns say that if you can recall an Usher's face clearly afterward, it's because they wanted you to.

They carry small notebooks that always seem to already know your ring name, your Renown, and your infractions. A lone Usher is rare. A gathering of seven is the worst thing that can happen to you in the Backstage.

Wherever an Usher enters a scene, the House Difficulty automatically hardens (see below) — mortals nearby suddenly remember somewhere else to be, and Clowns feel an unplaceable weight in their chest, as if the Backstage itself were embarrassed of them.

## Ranks

1. **Ushers** — the base, most numerous rank. Patrol low-Suspicion scenes, issue formal warnings, erase minor mortal memories, and escort infractors to speak with someone higher up.
2. **Régisseurs** — mid-rank coordinators. Appear once Suspicion has spiraled; can rewrite dozens of mortal memories at once, and can impose formal sanctions on infracting Clowns (revoking Threshold access, freezing a Motley "under review," dampening Laughter generation for a season). They negotiate first, more often than not.
3. **Stage Directors** — rare and very dangerous. Appear only when something in the Backstage risks breaking for real, or when a Troupe is growing too fast. Can strip Renown from an infractor, force an Incorporation into a specific body, or seal an entire Threshold.
4. **The Head Régisseur** — rumored, unconfirmed. Said to be singular, and never seen clearly by anyone who lived to describe it well. Manifests, if it manifests at all, only when the Backstage itself is at existential risk. Campaign-climax material, not a standard encounter.

## Their Blind Spot

The Ushers exist to manage the spectacle, but **they do not process the Flop** — a failure that is also a success. It doesn't fit their categories. A plan built around deliberate Flops (the School of Failure, the Augustes, any well-timed Creative Gambit) is the closest thing to a reliable weapon against them.

## Running Them at the Table

**When they appear** — driven by the Suspicion in a scene or location:

| Suspicion | Typical response |
|---|---|
| Low (0–2) | Nothing yet |
| Medium (3–5) | An Usher may appear with a formal warning |
| High (6–8) | One or two Régisseurs intervene directly |
| Critical (9+) | A Stage Director manifests, with an Usher escort |

**While present:** House Difficulty hardens by −1 per Usher present. Failed Bits in front of an Usher generate +1 extra Suspicion. The Flop still works normally against them — their one real weakness.

**Their stats and resources:** they have Laughter but rarely spend it, favoring fixed **Protocols** instead of Turns (no Bit needed — they're already mundane by design). Their Face is unusually high (typically 10, even for base Ushers) because they aren't people — they're pure function, and function doesn't hesitate. **Emptying an Usher's Face doesn't turn them into an NPC — it dissolves them**, and the Backstage quietly reassigns the role to another anonymous body elsewhere. This is the only way to remove one for good. Renown scales by rank: Usher 4–5, Régisseur 7–8, Stage Director 9–10, Head Régisseur 10+.

**Sample Protocols:**

| Protocol | Minimum rank | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Formal Warning | Usher | Marks the target "under observation" — the MC gains +1 permanent Suspicion on them for the session |
| Memory Correction | Usher | Erases the last minute of a mortal's memory |
| Escort to the Office | Usher | Forces a lone Clown to come along; resisting starts a fight |
| Threshold Closure | Régisseur | Temporarily seals a Threshold |
| Administrative Sanction | Régisseur | A lasting debuff — losing access to a Path Grade for a session, halved Laughter generation, or similar |
| Renown Reduction | Stage Director | Temporarily lowers a target's effective Renown by 1–3 |
| Motley Reassignment | Stage Director | Forces Incorporation of a possessed Motley into a specific body |
| Closure | Head Régisseur | Campaign-climax material — can erase a whole location, or make a Clown vanish entirely from the world's memory |

**No party is meant to beat a swarm of them alone.** Party coordination, shared Momentum, and deliberate Flops are the intended counters. Once a Stage Director shows up, the smart move is often to negotiate or flee, not escalate.

## Mundane Threats (no Backstage ties)

**Nervous Security Guard** *(trivial)* — Pool 3, Health 2. Calls for backup before engaging directly. *Hook:* the real danger is Suspicion from a badly sold Bit, not the guard himself.

**Neighborhood Tough** *(standard)* — Pool 6, Health 4. Hits hard, no tactics, never coordinates with nearby toughs. *Hook:* vulnerable to any distraction or con — never expects a clown to be dangerous.

**Officer of Uncertain Loyalty** *(standard, built-in hook)* — Pool 6, Health 3. Can call in backup in 2–3 rounds if not stopped or won over. *Hook:* this same block can be an ordinary cop, an Usher in disguise, or a mole inside a Rebel Clown Army cell — reuse it for any of the three without tipping your hand.

**Obsessed Fan / Tabloid Reporter** *(social threat, no meaningful Health)* — Pool 5 (Persuasion or Subterfuge). Each unresolved scene with them adds +1 cumulative Suspicion. *Hook:* almost always tied to the New Circus (real mortal fame); a well-sold Bit neutralizes them without violence.

## Minor Backstage Echoes (non-Clown supernatural threats)

**Corpsing Echo** *(unsettling, Renown-equivalent 2)* — Pool 4, Health 2. A manifestation of a Bit that failed catastrophically and never faded — the literal seed of mortal fear of clowns. Terrifies more than it wounds. *Hook:* dissolves if someone lands a well-sold Flop in its presence — a genuine laugh literally unmakes it. The Rebel Clown Army and the School of Failure handle these almost effortlessly.

**Whisper of an Orphaned Motley** *(subtle, Renown-equivalent 3, Health 1)* — Pool 3 (mental effects only, no physical attack). The residual echo of an unclaimed Motley. Whispers temptations of non-consensual Incorporation to anyone who lingers nearby. *Hook:* high Awareness catches it before it acts; a great way to introduce an interesting Motley without a full dead Clown attached yet.

**Hollow Applause Spectre** *(Renown-equivalent 4)* — Pool 5, Health 3. Born from mass laughter with nothing real behind it. Steals 1–2 Laughter on contact. *Hook:* almost exclusive to New Circus territory; a Physician or Host generating real Laughter nearby repels it.

**Cut Puppet** *(feral construct, Renown-equivalent 2–3)* — Pool 4, Health 2. A summoned construct that lost its caster and wandered off, hostile by default. *Hook:* no real malice — surprisingly easy to calm with the right Path (Host, Physician) instead of destroying.

## Rival Clowns (template + ready examples)

Use the scaling table above to improvise any rival on the fly. Three ready examples follow.

**"Cabbage" — street Auguste** *(Renown 2, minor)* — Path: Pickpocket (Zanni), Grade I. Health 3. Laughter 4/10. Face 6/10. Gifts: Weighted Pie... no — *Slippery Hands, Look Over There, Set the Trick.* *Hook:* steals out of need, not malice — almost always resolved by talking or intimidation rather than a fight. A good first Clown-on-Clown encounter for a new party.

**"The Crimson Shadow" — School of Failure Pickpocket** *(Renown 5, standard-strong)* — Path: Pickpocket (Blade), Grade III. Health 5. Laughter 7/10. Face 7/10. Ambushing the ambusher flips the fight.

**"Monsignor Rictus" — a fallen Whiteface, an oath broken** *(Renown 7, mini-boss)* — A former rising Ring Legend who **broke his own Oath** for power and is stuck in permanent Oathbreaking — unable to generate Conviction, compensating with pure cruelty toward his old apprentices. Health 6. Laughter 9/10. Face 8/10 (fragile emotionally — see hook). Gifts: The Vow Made Force, Punishment, Sentence, Greater Punishment, Living Banner. *Hook:* his Aura is dead (Oathbreaking), so his followers stay out of fear, not loyalty, and can be turned mid-fight if offered a way out. Confronting him with proof of his own betrayal can break him emotionally before anyone lands a physical blow.

## Campaign Bosses

### "The Clown of a Thousand Faces" — an unstable, unclaimed Motley
**Origin:** an ancient, powerful Motley (historical Renown 9) that has passed through several failed Incorporations — every claimant ended up possessed, and the "Clown" wearing it now is an unstable fusion of every trapped personality inside it.
**Renown:** 9. Health 8. Laughter 10/10. Face 10/10 (split across phases).
**Phases:** *Phase 1 — One Face in Charge:* fights with the dominant personality's Gifts (recommended: Shackled, Grade IV–V). *Phase 2 — The Faces Turn on Each Other:* at half Health, the trapped personalities start fighting for control — random or contradictory effects, and a real window of vulnerability. *Phase 3 — The Original Face:* at low Face, the original, weaker personality briefly surfaces, pleading for help — the party can finish the fight, or attempt a real exorcism/Incorporation on the spot.
**Hook:** defeating it by Face instead of Health opens a genuinely different ending — freeing it, not just beating it.

### "Madame Ovation" — the producer who traded her soul for applause
**Origin:** a New Circus Ring Legend who chased the perfect spectacle for so long she can no longer generate real Laughter — only hollow applause, which she now has to steal from others to sustain herself.
**Renown:** 8. Health 7. Laughter 10/10 (stolen, not her own). Face 6/10 (more fragile than the shine suggests).
**Core trait:** constantly summons large-scale constructs (Balloon Elephants, swarms) as "supporting numbers," and her top-tier Gift drains Laughter from the whole party if left unchecked.
**Hook:** her low Face is the real weak point. A Physician or a member of the School of Failure who shows her real compassion — or confronts her with the truth — breaks her faster than any direct attack.

### The Head Régisseur *(rumor — campaign climax, no fixed stats)*
Deliberately unstatted. If it appears, it is the central event of an entire campaign, built to fit that specific story by the Master of Ceremonies. Its mere appearance should feel like the normal rules of the game bending.

# CHAPTER 11: HISTORY OF THE CRAFT

## Before the Ring Had a Name

Long before anyone called it the Backstage, the craft was already old. The first Threshold anyone can point to with any confidence sits in a palace that no longer stands, where a king once wrote a letter demanding that a dancer of unusual stature be brought to him at once, "to gladden the heart of the king" — and the messenger who carried that dancer north carried, without knowing it, the first recorded spark of what would one day become the Whiteface's claim to authority: the idea that laughter could be summoned, and that summoning it was a serious act of craft, not an accident.

From there the thread runs everywhere at once. Mimics working the market squares of old Greek colonies, wearing grotesque masks and playing gods as fools for a coin. A masked family of stock fools in southern Italy — the greedy old man, the glutton, the hunchbacked trickster — passed down through generations like a trade secret, because it was one. A stiff, wide-eyed idiot in a patchwork coat, built specifically to be shouted at and struck, standing in every Roman street show as the person everyone in the crowd was allowed, for one afternoon, to feel superior to.

## The Fool Who Was Licensed to Speak

In the halls of medieval kings, two kinds of fool sat at the foot of the throne, and the difference between them still shapes the craft today. One was kept because the world had already broken something in him, and a broken thing near a king was thought to carry a kind of accidental holiness. The other chose the mask — trained in music, in tumbling, in the exact art of saying an unbearable truth in a voice soft enough that a king could laugh instead of ordering a sword drawn.

That second fool is the ancestor the Whiteface still claims out loud: authority earned, not given by accident of birth, permission to speak the truth granted by the very power it needled — right up until the day it wasn't. Every fool in every court eventually learned exactly where that permission ended, and not one of them ever got to choose when.

## The Fraternity of Padua

On a February morning in 1545, in a small Italian city, a group of performers signed their names to a piece of paper and declared themselves a company — not a troupe assembled for one lord's wedding and dissolved after, but a trade, a business, a fraternity of the craft. That single piece of paper is the oldest confirmed proof that anyone treated clowning as a serious, organized profession, and every Troupe in the Backstage today, whether they admit it or not, is a descendant of the impulse behind it.

Out of that fraternity came the great cast of masks: the clever servant who always outsmarted his masters, the fat old miser, the pompous doctor who knew nothing, the cowardly captain who talked like a hero. And out of one servant mask in particular — soft-featured, a little sad, always the second choice next to the clever one — came a figure who crossed into France, lost his sharp wit somewhere on the journey, and gained a white face and a broken heart instead. He became the model for every quiet, melancholy performer who ever chose silence as an art form, and everyone who has ever built a Motley around grief instead of noise owes him something.

## The Ring Is Drawn

For most of the craft's life, there was no ring — only stages, squares, and courts. The ring itself, the circle a Clown works inside today, was drawn for the first time by a horseman who discovered that a rider standing on a galloping horse needed exactly one shape of arena to keep his balance, and built his whole business around it. Once the ring existed, it needed something between the trick riders to keep the crowd from losing interest, and so a tailor was invented who couldn't ride a horse to save his life, fell off it eleven separate ways, and always, at the very last moment, revealed that he could ride perfectly all along.

One performer more than any other decided what a Clown should look like doing that: white paint over the whole face and throat, two sharp red marks on the cheeks, an expression built to read from the back row by candlelight. He never once worked inside the ring — his whole career was on a stage — and it didn't matter. His face became the face, so completely that for a hundred years afterward, "one of his kind" was simply the word for any Clown at all.

## The One Who Fell

Every Whiteface needs someone to laugh at, and the story the Troupe tells about how that someone was born has been told so many times that no two versions agree. A young performer, the story goes, was locked in his dressing room as punishment for missing his entrance, and out of boredom put on ill-fitting clothes and mimicked the man who'd locked him in. Caught red-handed, he fled onto the very stage he wasn't allowed near, tripped going in, tripped coming out, and the crowd — cruel, delighted, unstoppable — gave him a name on the spot that stuck to an entire tradition.

Maybe that's exactly what happened. Maybe it isn't. What's certain is that within a few decades, every whiteface figure in every ring had a red-nosed counterpart beside him, built to receive the pie, the slap, the bucket of water — the one who was, by the old rules of the craft, not even allowed to call himself a Clown at all, only the one the Clown happened to. Some of the most famous pairs to ever work a ring were built on a real and ugly imbalance of power between the two halves — one man giving the orders and the blows, the other required by his contract to take them and smile. That imbalance is still folded into the bones of the Whiteface and Auguste's relationship today, and neither Troupe pretends otherwise anymore.

## The One Who Rode the Rails

Not every sad face in the ring was born from heartbreak. One of them was born from something far uglier: a double act, built by two performers to caricature the poorest and most desperate people of their era, that leaned without apology on the cruelest theatrical habits of its time. That is a real part of the lineage the Tramp carries, and the Troupe does not get to pretend it isn't.

What happened next is why the Tramp survived at all. Decades later, in the hardest years anyone in the country could remember, a different performer built a small, wordless, endlessly unlucky figure out of that same rough shape — patched coat, broken hat, a face that had given up trying to win — and something in it landed differently the second time. Audiences who had lost everything looked at a man who lost everything on stage, over and over, and never once stopped getting back up, and they recognized themselves in him instead of laughing down at him. When fire once tore through a circus tent full of people, that same performer was seen afterward, still in full paint, carrying a bucket of water toward the flames because there wasn't time to change out of the part he was playing. The Tramp Troupe holds both of those stories at once, on purpose, because both of them are true, and it has never fully decided what to do with the space between them.

## The One Who Left the Ring for the Stage

One performer refused to stay in either the ring or the sad-tramp shadow of it. Trained to play a dozen instruments badly on purpose, he took his act off the sawdust and onto proper theater stages across an entire continent, insisting the whole time that clowning could stand shoulder to shoulder with any other serious performing art. He was, for a while, the highest-paid performer in his part of the world, and every Clown since who has ever tried to argue that the craft deserves a real stage and real respect is quoting him, whether they know it or not.

## The School of the Fall

Much later, in a small studio far from any ring, a teacher began doing something strange: making trained actors fail, on purpose, in front of each other, over and over, until something honest broke loose in them that no amount of good acting ever could. His own student went further, building an entire school around one blunt idea — the fool doesn't make the audience laugh at a joke, he makes them laugh at watching someone believe, completely and disastrously, in a terrible idea. That lineage runs, almost without translation, straight into everything the School of Failure believes today, including its uncomfortable honesty about how easily that same teaching can slide into real cruelty when a master forgets where the line is.

## The Doctor at the Bedside

Somewhere around the same years, in hospital wards nobody expected to hear laughter in, a different kind of Clown started showing up — not to headline anything, but to sit with people no one else knew how to sit with. One of them, after his own brother died, said that a single visit to a children's ward gave him the fullest twenty minutes of his entire career, and built a whole program around finding out if he was right. He was blunt about the difference between what he was doing and grander claims other people made about clowning-as-medicine: *he* was just a professional fool, doing a fool's real job in a place that badly needed one. A doctor once told one of his performers, coldly, that clowns didn't belong in a hospital. She told him neither did children. That exchange is close enough to scripture for the Laughter Doctors that most of them can recite it from memory.

## The Flock

More recently — recently enough that some of the people who did it are still walking around in the Backstage today — a different kind of Clown decided the craft belonged in the street, in front of riot shields, at the gates of the powerful. They dressed half like soldiers and half like a birthday party on purpose, kissed the shields they were supposed to be afraid of, and left the people meant to control them radioing back in genuine, bewildered panic that "the clowns are organizing." It worked exactly as well as absurdity can work against something that has no sense of humor about itself, which is to say: sometimes brilliantly, sometimes not at all, and always at real personal risk to everyone brave enough to try it. The Rebel Clown Army carries that whole complicated legacy — the triumphs and the burnout and the informants — as recent history, not myth.

## The Big Top, Reimagined

The newest chapter is also the strangest one. A wave of performers, many of them former street artists, decided the circus deserved to be theater — no animals, no sawdust ring, real narrative, real choreography, real prestige. They got exactly what they wanted, on a scale no clowning tradition had ever reached before, playing to audiences the old ring could never have dreamed of. And then, slowly, almost without anyone deciding it on purpose, the very tradition that grew out of clowning began giving the clown less and less room inside its own biggest shows. The New Circus lives inside that irony every single day: the most successful heirs of the craft, quietly worried they've traded away the thing that made it worth inheriting.

## A Note on Fear

Somewhere in among all of this, in the last handful of decades, a very different story took root in the mortal world: the idea that a Clown might be something to be afraid of. No old tradition sits behind that fear. It has no ring, no teacher, no lineage. What it has, more likely, is generations of half-seen accidents — a Bit that failed badly, a moment when the mortal world caught a glimpse of something it wasn't supposed to see and never got a clean explanation for it — curdling slowly into a modern nightmare that borrowed the craft's face and kept none of its history. Every old Troupe agrees on very little. They agree on that.

# APPENDIX: GLOSSARY

- **Act** — a block of scenes forming one arc within a Show.
- **Backstage** — the hidden realm layered beneath the mortal world.
- **Bit** — the mundane cover story sold to mortals for a real Turn (see Double Vision).
- **Cheers / Jeers** — the two results of the House Die.
- **Conviction** — the Sworn's resource, earned by honoring an Oath in play.
- **Corpsing** — a Failure result paired with Jeers; the worst outcome on the Result Matrix.
- **Creative Gambit** — spending a bold, table-approved idea for +2 dice before a roll, at the risk of extra Face loss on failure.
- **Debt** — the Shackled's resource, a fiat payment that a Patron eventually collects.
- **Double Vision** — the rule that every supernatural act has a Turn (real effect) and a Bit (mortal cover).
- **Encore** — spending Laughter after a roll to reroll up to 3 failed dice.
- **Face** — the existential resource; hitting 0 traps a character permanently behind the mask.
- **First of May / Tent Veteran / Ring Name / Backstage Legend / Ring Legend** — the five Renown ranks, from novice to legend.
- **Flop** — a Failure result paired with Cheers; a glorious failure that can generate Laughter.
- **Gift** — an active ability granted by a Path.
- **House Die / House Difficulty** — the d12 rolled alongside every action pool, and the modifier the MC sets for how easy a given audience is to win over.
- **Renown** — a character's level and legend, rated 1–10.
- **Incorporation** — permanently claiming a dead Clown's Motley, risking Possession.
- **Intermission** — the downtime between sessions or major Acts.
- **Laughter** — the tactical resource that fuels every Gift.
- **Momentum** — the party's shared resource, spent to boost or coordinate.
- **Motley** — a Clown's costume and persona; can be gifted, seized, or inherited.
- **Path** — a character's class (Host, Illusionist, Shackled, Physician, Pickpocket, Strongman, Sworn).
- **Possession** — when Incorporation fails and the original owner of a Motley takes control.
- **Scene** — a unit of focused dramatic action.
- **Seizing a Motley** — taking a defeated Clown's Motley after emptying their Face (not just their Health).
- **Show** — a full session of play.
- **Spotlight** — the narrative mechanism that replaces fixed turn order.
- **Suspicion** — the MC's shared resource, raised by Jeers and Corpsing.
- **Taking the Face** — putting on makeup and costume to access a character's Gifts.
- **Threshold** — a place where a real performance once happened, allowing passage into the Backstage.
- **Troupe** — a character's tradition (Whiteface, Auguste, School of Failure, Laughter Doctors, Rebel Clown Army, Tramp, New Circus).
- **Turn** — the real supernatural effect behind a Bit (see Double Vision).
- **Ushers** — the Backstage's institutional antagonist, an impersonal maintenance force.
